Enhancing Communication for Non-Verbal MND Patients with AIConnect

1. Introduction 2. Problem Exploration 3. Design Specifications 4. AI Connect - The Solution 5. Product Prototyping 6. Shortcomings of Proposed System 7. Future Plans References

Non-speaking individuals use Augmentative Alternative Communication (AAC) to communicate with caregivers. AAC interventions have shown benefits, but limitations exist in usability and daily integration.

To empathize with the non-speaking community, I partnered with organizations supporting individuals with Autism, Down Syndrome, Dementia, and Motor Neurone Disease. Interviews with speech therapists and teaching staff provided deeper insights into current communication tools, their effectiveness, and suitability for each group.

For the individuals I have met, those with Autism, Down syndrome, and Dementia use low-tech AAC to communicate, while individuals with Motor Neurone Disease use high-tech AAC.

Exploration of different AAC tools and their challenges in assisting various user groups with communication impairments.

Challenges Low-tech AAC involves guessing to interpret meaning, requires caregiver attention, and frequent prompting. Eye-Gaze technology is challenging in bright environments and can be slow.

Given their potential to benefit significantly from AI-enhanced AAC, Motor Neurone Disease was chosen as the primary target group.

Physical Symptoms Speech: Loss of useful speech.

Movement:
  • Complete paralysis, rendering them unable to move any part of their body except for their eyes.
Sensory:
  • Often felt numb, needed caregivers to help in frequent repositioning.
Respiratory:
  • Needed frequent swabbing from caregivers due to excessive saliva production, which causes choking when not cleared in a timely manner.
Mode of Communication Indirect Access: Individuals with MND are unable to access laptops directly.

They use Eye Gaze Technology, Tobii Eye Tracker 5 to access the keyboard function on their laptop. This is paired with the Microsoft Assistive Keyboard available on their Windows Laptop.

Eye Gaze Technology Usage: The Tobii Eye Tracker 5, like many commercial eye-gaze devices, uses Infrared Oculography (IROG). This technology uses infrared light to illuminate the eye and photo detectors to capture the reflection. By measuring reflections from the eyeball and tracking the boundary between the pupil and iris, it calculates eye rotation to determine input. [1]

Microsoft Assistive Keyboard Usage:
Dwell Time: Users can select letters or commands by fixating their gaze on them for a specific duration, allowing hands-free interaction.
Commonly Typed Words Bar: The keyboard highlights frequently used words based on user history, increasing typing efficiency.
Speak Button: A "Speak" button allows users to have the generated text read aloud by a synthesized voice for effective communication.

A minority of elderly non-verbal patients use picture card systems, focusing on images to communicate preferences. This is due to Microsoft Assistive Keyboard’s lack of dialect support and the elderly’s unfamiliarity with typing, making picture cards a more reliable option.

Design problems, value propositions, and product design strategies.

Problems to Address

This is the list of problems, due to their physical symptoms and the gap in current Assistive Technology (AT) for the Target Audience (TA):

  1. External Factors cause frequent interruption to TA’s communication, causing a high need of recalibration. Most TA’s rely on their caregivers for recalibration on their remote eye tracking system, as the TAs’ eyes are already out of alignment, leading to difficulty for them to navigate to the recalibration page solely using eye movement.

External Factors Reason behind why
Sunlight and lighting changes. From observations, TA has reported high levels of inaccuracies when typing, outdoors. Reporting a number of 4 in 10 letters per word being mistyped, due to the inaccuracies that arise when typing outdoors. Sunlight has a critical impact on data captured by the eye tracker as infrared radiation from sunlight can cause corneal reflection signals to be hard to maintain and detect.[1][2] Additionally, sunlight reflecting off the skin, or eyes into the photo detectors can be mistaken by the eye-tracker as a corneal reflection, causing a reduction in spatial accuracy. [1]
Other individuals are within the range of detection. During observations, it was noted that caregivers were frequently detected within the camera's detection range as they attempted to complete partially composed utterances on the TA's AAC, given their visual access to the device. Similar behavior has been documented in previous studies.[2] Accidental mismeasurements can happen, where the eye tracker senses another pair of eyes. [1]
Slight and Major movement of TA from original position. During observations, TA often had to readjust their positions, as they suffered from numbness at times. Additionally, daily activities such as going to the toilet can cause TA to shift their position. Research has shown that slight movements in head can also lead to errors in gaze location estimates, leading to inaccuracies and the need for recalibration. [3]

  1. Slow communication by TA as TA tends to mistype letters occasionally. This is due to the natural, rapid movements of the eyes called saccades, as well as eye drifts—small, involuntary shifts away from the fixation point—causing errors with fixations to select a particular letter.[2]

    Additionally, eye trackers exhibit systematic errors, the disparity between the average gazepoint location and actual fixation by TA, causing a lack in accuracy. Eye movement data is inherently noisy, causing the calibration to deteriorate over the course of use per time. [1]

  2. Eye fatigue experienced by TA while communicating.

    Eye fatigue experienced is commonly misattributed to tired ocular muscles, when it is in fact the result of tired eyelid muscles, with our ocular muscles being strong and precise. [1] As such, user-friendly interfaces should be utilized to combat eye fatigue, such as using screens of darker shades, and having shorter gaze durations (dwell times).

The proposed product aims to provide real-time communication, reliable calibration, and increased independence.

This project aims to create a user-friendly, reliable, and efficient AAC solution for MND patients.
